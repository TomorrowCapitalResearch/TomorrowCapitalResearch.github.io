I. Strategic Framework

The imperative for grand challenges in quantitative finance

The trajectory of scientific progress has often been shaped by the deliberate pursuit of profound, unanswered questions. David Hilbert’s famous list of problems and, more recently, the Clay Mathematics Institute’s Millennium Prize Problems, concentrated intellectual effort on foundational hurdles and reshaped entire fields.

The resolution of the Poincaré conjecture, first posed in 1904, is a canonical example: beyond solving a single puzzle, it stimulated decades of research and redefined the landscape of topology. These initiatives preserve the integrity of fundamental inquiry by setting clear, ambitious, technically rigorous targets.

Financial markets, by contrast, are highly complex, non-stationary adaptive systems. Progress in quantitative finance cannot rely solely on incremental algorithmic improvements or isolated empirical “edges”. Genuine advancement requires confronting fundamental theoretical limits on:

  • Market microstructure and order-book dynamics
  • Non-linearity, path dependence, and roughness
  • Adversarial and competitive interactions between agents
  • Informational efficiency, learning, and regime shifts

Other domains, such as global health and climate, have successfully used “Grand Challenge” frameworks to align capital, talent, and institutions around their hardest problems. Quantitative and algorithmic finance still lacks a comparably coherent set of formally posed, foundational problems.

Tomorrow’s Conjectures are our attempt to supply this. By framing a small number of central questions as formal conjectures, Tomorrow Capital Research (TCR) aims to distinguish verifiable intellectual breakthroughs from transient performance gains tied to specific regimes or datasets. The focus is on breakthroughs that yield provable computational or game-theoretic solutions, with clear paths to implementation.

As quants, we also know that the pursuit of deep, grand problems—with the intention to understand and improve upon the structure of markets—is the driving force for many of us. Tomorrow’s Conjectures are intended as shared targets for that ambition.

I.B. Nomenclature & rewards

Why “conjectures”? What is awarded?

The semantic choice between “postulate” and “conjecture” is deliberate.

A Postulate (or Axiom) is a fundamental assumption that defines the rules of a mathematical system and is accepted without proof. Non-Euclidean geometries, for example, arise by modifying a single Euclidean postulate. Such statements are not posed as challenges; accepting them as given would be counterproductive when our aim is to invite solutions.

A Conjecture, by contrast, is a precise statement believed to be true (or false) but not yet proven. The purpose of Tomorrow’s Conjectures is precisely to invite the community to:

  • Produce a formal proof that resolves the conjecture and elevates it to the status of a theorem, or
  • Provide a decisive counterexample or re-formulation, supported by algorithmic implementation and empirical validation.

For each of the five conjectures, TCR intends to award successful resolutions with:

  • A monetary prize (size to be announced)
  • The Tomorrow Medal, our highest formal recognition for theoretical achievement
  • Endorsement and amplification of the work as a landmark result, shared as widely as possible across academic and industry networks

A successful submission must meet rigorous standards comparable to top-tier academic research in quantitative finance. Three components are required:

  • Formal mathematical statement. A rigorous proof demonstrating the existence, uniqueness, or key properties of the solution within a clearly defined model—or a decisive counterexample that falsifies or reformulates the conjecture.
  • Verifiable algorithm. A computationally tractable algorithm derived from the formal result, with sufficient detail for independent implementation and analysis.
  • Comprehensive validation. Exhaustive testing against public and, where possible, blinded out-of-sample datasets using transparent, statistically sound methodologies that guard against overfitting and regime-specific artifacts.
II. The five conjectures

Formal formulation of Tomorrow’s Conjectures

The five conjectures outlined below are intentionally diverse in scope: from regime detection and portfolio optimization to execution, dynamic ownership, and multi-player strategic equilibrium. Each targets a structural limit or friction within algorithmic finance and requires both theoretical and practical resolution.

Conjecture 1: The Robust Market Regime Engine (The MRE Problem)

Regime Detection · Speed–Accuracy Trade-off

Challenge statement

Design and formally validate a Market Regime Engine (MRE) that optimizes a real-time Speed–Accuracy Trade-Off function, providing the highest classification fidelity of latent market states (volatility, trend, segmentation) subject to strict computational latency constraints (e.g., microseconds–milliseconds).

Formalizing regime detection and the SAT constraint

Market regimes represent persistent states of a financial market, such as high-volatility episodes or sustained directional trends. Accurately classifying these regimes is critical, as they condition the relevance of factors and the expected performance of trading strategies.

In practice, regime detection frequently relies on unsupervised or semi-supervised methods, such as Hidden Markov Models, Gaussian Mixture Models, or advanced clustering (e.g., Wasserstein k-means). However, financial return series present challenges:

  • Heavy-tailed distributions and sensitivity to outliers
  • Temporal dependence and autocorrelation
  • Non-stationarity and regime transitions

A successful MRE must rigorously define the latent state space and provide a classification method that jointly handles distributional properties and temporal dependence. The defining constraint is the speed–accuracy trade-off: complex models may improve theoretical accuracy but introduce latency that renders decisions useless in high-frequency contexts.

The conjecture seeks to maximize a generalized utility function defined along an acceptable Speed–Accuracy Trade-Off curve, where accuracy (e.g., F1-score, log-likelihood) is penalized by a latency-dependent term that enforces a strict upper bound on decision time. The optimal solution identifies the balance point that maximizes utility under real-time constraints.

Required output and success metrics

  • Primary metric: Area Under the Curve (AUC) of the generalized utility function over accuracy–latency space, evaluated specifically within the region that satisfies the real-time latency ceiling.
  • Secondary metric: Statistical superiority of the MRE’s regime transition model in explaining and forecasting volatility clustering and return dynamics, compared against established unsupervised baselines (e.g., HMMs, GMMs).

Conjecture 2: The Constrained Optimization Frontier (The COF Problem)

Portfolio Optimization · Liquidity & Factors

Challenge statement

Develop a generalized, tractable framework for convex portfolio optimization under uncertainty that simultaneously incorporates non-linear market frictions—specifically dynamic liquidation speed capacity and factor-adjusted diversification—while achieving a defined, stable return/risk profile.

Integrating constraints and frictions

Classical Modern Portfolio Theory focuses on variance minimization and typically ignores key real-world frictions: liquidity limits, execution capacity, and factor concentration. Practical portfolio construction must embed:

  • Position limits, leverage and budget constraints
  • Liquidity & liquidation speed constraints
  • Factor and sector concentration controls

Liquidation speed capacity

Liquidation Speed Capacity models the relationship between portfolio size, desired changes in asset weights, and available market volume. For asset i, with average daily volume ADVi, the change in exposure over a rebalancing window must respect a maximum participation rate λmax, ensuring that rebalancing does not create undue market impact.

The conjecture requires solving for the maximum achievable, stable return/risk ratio subject to a fixed, constrained liquidation capacity, demonstrating resilience under liquidation stress scenarios.

Factor-adjusted diversification

Simple concentration metrics like the Herfindahl–Hirschman Index (HHI) consider only position sizes. True diversification, however, depends critically on the correlation structure between assets and their exposure to common risk factors.

The conjecture mandates a generalized HHI that incorporates either the asset covariance matrix or factor loadings, penalizing highly correlated exposures more aggressively than uncorrelated ones. The goal is to find weight vectors that satisfy:

  • A target constrained return/risk profile, and
  • A provably optimal factor-adjusted diversification score, under liquidity and participation constraints.

The central challenge is preserving mathematical tractability (e.g., convexity) in the presence of such non-linear frictions.

Required output and success metrics

  • Primary metric: An efficiency ratio defined as the constrained return/risk (e.g., Sharpe Ratio) divided by the theoretical unconstrained optimum. Maximizing this ratio demonstrates efficient performance under stringent liquidity and factor constraints.
  • Secondary metric: A factor-adjusted diversification score statistically higher than those achieved by portfolios optimized only under classical MPT variance minimization, demonstrating superior factor-level risk mitigation.

Conjecture 3: The Cryptographic Execution Problem (The CXP Problem)

Execution · Game Theory · Stealth

Challenge statement

Derive the optimal order distribution strategy for large-volume execution in a competitive market, framed as a game-theoretic problem, where the strategy maximizes expected P&L by minimizing informational leakage and subsequent alpha decay induced by competing agents.

Execution as a game

Traditional optimal execution focuses on minimizing implementation shortfall by balancing permanent and temporary impact against holding risk. In modern electronic markets, this is insufficient: other algorithmic participants actively observe and respond to order flow.

The execution agent’s strategy must therefore be optimal not only with respect to impact, but also with respect to strategic detection and exploitation by other self-interested agents. This places the problem squarely within algorithmic game theory, where utility depends on both one’s own strategy and the distribution of opponents’ strategies.

Modeling stealth and information leakage

The conjecture requires a formal market model in which:

  • The execution agent’s order flow generates signals observable to competing algorithms.
  • Competing agents attempt to infer latent order intent and front-run or otherwise exploit the detected pattern.
  • The execution agent’s utility depends on implementation shortfall and an explicit alpha decay term linked to detectability.

The goal is to solve for a (possibly mixed) strategy that minimizes implementation shortfall while maximizing time-to-detection or minimizing detection probability, within a Nash or mean-field game equilibrium framework.

Required output and success metrics

  • Primary metric: Percentage reduction in implementation shortfall achieved by the derived strategy relative to baseline methods (e.g., VWAP, TWAP), conditional on a quantifiable stealth measure such as detection entropy of the order stream.
  • Secondary metric: Existence and construction of a stable, computationally feasible Nash (or mean-field) equilibrium in a simulated multi-agent market with adaptive competitors, together with empirical validation of its robustness.

Conjecture 4: The Dynamic Ownership Paradigm (The DOP Problem)

Asset Allocation · Microstructure

Challenge statement

Formulate a dynamic asset control policy that determines, at each time step, whether an asset should be held (passive allocation) or actively traded, based solely on objective time-series features of its market microstructure—specifically market capitalization, trading volume, and inferred ownership structure.

The Hold vs. Trade decision boundary

Dynamic Asset Allocation adjusts weights in response to evolving conditions. The Dynamic Ownership Paradigm introduces a binary decision for each asset:

  • Trade: trigger active rebalancing, incurring transaction costs
  • Hold: maintain current weight, incurring only holding risk

The decision boundary must be defined purely by microstructure variables, reflecting the trade-off between expected benefit of active management and its cost, under realistic liquidity and ownership conditions.

Microstructure inputs and ownership inference

The optimal policy should use:

  • Market cap and trading volume as proxies for liquidity and depth
  • Inferred institutional ownership structure from block trade patterns, volume profiles, and trade size distributions

Because ownership data is often delayed or incomplete, the solution must provide a robust inference mechanism. Different ownership regimes (e.g., institutional dominance vs. retail-driven flow) imply different impact profiles and hence different thresholds for when active trading is justified.

Required output and success metrics

  • Primary metric: Maximization of a Sortino Ratio-like measure adjusted for turnover, where downside risk is penalized and high turnover is explicitly and significantly penalized in the denominator. The policy must demonstrate true capital efficiency, not just raw returns.
  • Secondary metric: Statistical proof that active decisions are invoked only when the predicted improvement in risk/return trade-off reliably exceeds estimated transaction and impact costs for the asset’s inferred ownership regime.

Conjecture 5: The Multiplayer Strategic Equilibrium (The MSE Problem)

Multi-Agent Games · Pareto Optimality

Challenge statement

For any complex, partially observable, stochastic strategy game involving many rational players, define and solve for a Pareto optimal strategy set by proposing a three-part strategy framework (Information, Actions, Goals) that robustly integrates communication and alliance formation to achieve globally maximized utility, even when individual goals conflict.

The multi-agent strategy challenge

AI research has progressed from two-player perfect information games to multi-agent, partially observable environments such as StarCraft II and Diplomacy. In such games, success is fundamentally about coordinating beliefs, communication, and commitments, not merely about selecting moves in isolation.

In financial analogues—large hedge funds and trading firms competing for alpha—the highest collective outcome often lies on the Pareto frontier, where no agent can improve their utility without harming another. Yet, individual incentives and equilibrium concepts like Nash often favor defection over cooperation.

Formal 3-part strategy structure

The conjecture requires a formal algorithmic framework decomposed into:

  • Information: protocols for state observation, belief propagation, and communication, including strategic deception and signaling.
  • Actions: both standard game actions and meta-actions related to negotiation, alliance formation, commitments, and sanctions.
  • Goals: explicit representation of individual utility functions and collective objectives.

The core requirement is a mechanism, built from these components, that allows agents to predict and influence one another’s beliefs and to stably coordinate on cooperative solutions.

Pareto optimal equilibrium requirement

The ultimate breakthrough sought is a constructive proof of existence and an algorithm for attaining a Pareto optimal strategy set in complex, multi-player strategy spaces. The algorithm must demonstrate that rational, self-interested agents can systematically coordinate to achieve outcomes that are strictly superior to Nash equilibria in terms of global utility.

Required output and success metrics

  • Primary metric: Average percentage of global maximum attainable utility achieved by the proposed strategies across a diverse benchmark suite of strategic games or financial competition simulations. The solution must consistently deliver super-Nash collective outcomes.
  • Secondary metric: Formal verification of the game-theoretic structure in a machine-checkable proof language (e.g., Lean), establishing mathematical rigor for both the strategy framework and the associated equilibrium proofs.
III. Implementation

Adjudication and governance framework

Scientific Advisory Board

The long-term credibility of Tomorrow’s Conjectures depends on an uncompromising standard of adjudication, paralleling governance models used by institutions such as the Clay Mathematics Institute.

Tomorrow Capital Research will establish a Scientific Advisory Board (SAB) composed of:

  • Leading academics in mathematics and computer science
  • Researchers in quantitative finance and market microstructure
  • Practitioners experienced in algorithmic trading, execution, and risk management

The SAB is responsible for preserving the integrity and spirit of the conjectures, defining and updating award rules, and verifying that any claimed solution satisfies the stated standards.

Submission requirements

Submissions will be welcomed from university students, independent researchers, and quantitative professionals. Each submission must include:

  • A research paper (e.g., 5–7 pages) summarizing the contribution, providing context, and clearly stating the main results.
  • Complete technical documentation, including proofs, algorithms, and implementation details sufficient for independent reproduction.
  • A clear separation between in-sample development results and final out-of-sample validation, with reproducible code and experiment logs where applicable.

Scoring and validation criteria

Adjudication will weight scientific merit, innovation, and practical impact equally. A proposed scoring breakdown is:

Criterion Weight Description
Applicability & Relevance 30% Transformative utility and scalability for real-world capital management, systemic risk reduction, or market design.
Innovation & Novelty 30% Introduction of fundamentally new mathematical models, game-theoretic constructions, or algorithmic architectures.
Accuracy & Completeness 30% Quantitative precision, stability, statistical significance (especially out-of-sample), and logical completeness of proofs.
Formal Presentation & Clarity 10% Rigor of formulation, clarity of exposition, and adherence to academic standards.

Each conjecture also specifies its own validation metrics (e.g., AUC of the Speed–Accuracy trade-off curve, factor-adjusted diversification scores, detection entropy, Pareto efficiency measures), designed to move beyond simple P&L and capture efficiency, computational cost, and competitive robustness.

IV. Outlook

Conclusions and next steps

Tomorrow’s Conjectures are intended as a long-horizon, academically rigorous framework for directing future research in algorithmic finance. They are designed to attract not only quantitative finance specialists, but also researchers in computer science, game theory, and pure mathematics.

The required outputs intentionally go beyond backtests and empirical heuristics, demanding generalized, provable strategies that remain valid under realistic frictions and competitive dynamics. In this sense, Tomorrow’s Conjectures are closer to the Millennium Prize Problems than to a trading competition.

Final award of any prize money and conferment of the Tomorrow Medal will be conditional upon SAB verification that the solution meets the highest standards of proof, robustness, and reproducibility, and that it truly advances the theoretical framework of quantitative finance.

Details on formal submission procedures, timelines, and prize amounts will be published in a subsequent update. For expressions of interest or to be notified when submissions open, please contact Tomorrow Capital Research through our official channels.
